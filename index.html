  • Repeatable calibration
  • Thermal drift control
  • Robust operation in field conditions

How the components were selected?

1. Strain gauge

To reduce excitation power while maintaining good signal sensitivity, 1000 Î© quarter-bridge strain gauges were selected. Compared to typical 120 Î© / 350 Î© gauges, the higher resistance significantly reduces current draw, improving overall battery life without compromising measurement quality.

2. ADC + Amplification

For signal amplification and analog-to-digital conversion, the Texas Instruments 24-bit ADC was selected. This device integrates a high-resolution multi-channel ADC along with an internal programmable gain amplifier (PGA), making it well-suited for low-level strain gauge measurements.

The ADC was chosen primarily because it supports:

  • Simultaneous multi-channel sampling, enabling synchronised strain measurements across multiple sensors
  • High resolution conversion, allowing accurate capture of microvolt-level strain signals
  • Integrated PGA, reducing the need for external analog amplification stages
  • Low noise performance, improving measurement stability and repeatability
  • SPI digital interface, simplifying MCU communication and data acquisition

3. Microcontroller

The STM32 ultra-low-power microcontroller was selected as the main control unit of the strain gauge instrumentation system. It was responsible for coordinating data acquisition, managing system calibration, and implementing power-optimised operation for long-term field deployment.

The STM32 MCU was chosen primarily due to its:

  • Ultra-low power architecture, enabling extended battery life in remote environments
  • SPI peripheral support, required for reliable communication with the 24-bit ADC
  • Sufficient Flash and RAM, for firmware, calibration storage, and acquisition logic
  • Robust GPIO and timer peripherals, useful for system control, synchronization, and debugging

4. Excitation Source

A stable and low-noise excitation source was critical to ensure accurate and repeatable strain measurements, since any variation in bridge excitation directly affects the measured output voltage.

The excitation stage was implemented using a 3 V LDO regulator, which provided a clean and regulated excitation supply for the Wheatstone bridge. This ensured stable operation under battery voltage variation, reduced supply ripple, and improved overall measurement consistency.

Using the LDO-based excitation approach provided a reliable and low-noise bridge supply, supporting accurate strain measurements in harsh field conditions.
